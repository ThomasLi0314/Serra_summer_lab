Math Foundations

The geometry of finite-time transport: flow maps, deformation, and the variational theory behind LCSs.

1. Flow map and deformation gradient theory

Consider a two-dimensional, possibly time-dependent velocity field $\mathbf{v}: D \times [t_0, t_F] \to \mathbb{R}^2$. A fluid particle starting at $\mathbf{x}_0$ at time $t_0$ satisfies

$$ \dot{\mathbf{x}}(t) = \mathbf{v}(t,\mathbf{x}(t)), \qquad \mathbf{x}(t_0) = \mathbf{x}_0. $$

The flow map $F_{t_0}^{t}: D \to D$ sends each initial position to its image at time $t$:

$$ F_{t_0}^{t}(\mathbf{x}_0) \;=\; \mathbf{x}(t;\,t_0,\mathbf{x}_0). $$

The deformation gradient is the Jacobian of the flow map with respect to the initial condition:

$$ \nabla F_{t_0}^{t}(\mathbf{x}_0) \;=\; \frac{\partial F_{t_0}^{t}}{\partial \mathbf{x}_0} \;=\; \begin{pmatrix} \partial x(t)/\partial x_0 & \partial x(t)/\partial y_0 \\[2pt] \partial y(t)/\partial x_0 & \partial y(t)/\partial y_0 \end{pmatrix}. $$

An infinitesimal material segment $d\mathbf{x}_0$ is mapped to $d\mathbf{x}(t) = \nabla F \, d\mathbf{x}_0$, so the change of its squared length is

$$ \|d\mathbf{x}(t)\|^2 \;=\; d\mathbf{x}_0^{\!\top} \bigl(\nabla F^{\!\top} \nabla F\bigr) d\mathbf{x}_0 \;=\; d\mathbf{x}_0^{\!\top} \, C(\mathbf{x}_0) \, d\mathbf{x}_0. $$
Code note. LCStool never explicitly forms $F_{t_0}^t$; it computes $\nabla F$ either by finite differences on an auxiliary grid ('finiteDifference' mode in eig_cgStrain) or by integrating the equation of variation ('equationOfVariation' mode).

Equation of variation

Differentiating the ODE with respect to $\mathbf{x}_0$ gives a matrix ODE for $\Phi(t) := \nabla F_{t_0}^{t}$:

$$ \dot{\Phi}(t) = \nabla \mathbf{v}(t, \mathbf{x}(t))\,\Phi(t), \qquad \Phi(t_0) = I. $$

For a planar flow this is a $2\times 2$ system. LCStool packs it into the ODE state as $[x,\,y,\,\Phi_{11},\,\Phi_{12},\,\Phi_{21},\,\Phi_{22}]^\top$, yielding six coupled equations. This is the useEoV flag controlling whether extra states are carried. Look at the tail end of demo/double_gyre/derivative.m for a concrete example.

2. Cauchy-Green strain tensor theory

The Cauchy-Green strain tensor is the symmetric, positive-definite matrix

$$ C_{t_0}^{t}(\mathbf{x}_0) \;=\; \bigl[\nabla F_{t_0}^{t}(\mathbf{x}_0)\bigr]^{\!\top}\!\nabla F_{t_0}^{t}(\mathbf{x}_0) \;\in\; \mathbb{R}^{2\times 2}. $$

Its spectrum $\{\lambda_1, \lambda_2\}$ with $0 < \lambda_1 \le \lambda_2$ has orthonormal eigenvectors $\{\xi_1, \xi_2\}$:

$$ C \,\xi_i \;=\; \lambda_i \,\xi_i, \qquad \xi_1 \perp \xi_2, \qquad \lambda_1 \lambda_2 = \det C = (\det \nabla F)^2. $$

Because of the geometric meaning above, the stretch experienced by a material segment aligned with $\xi_i$ over $[t_0,t]$ is exactly $\sqrt{\lambda_i}$:

$$ \|\nabla F\,\xi_i\| \;=\; \sqrt{\lambda_i}. $$

Incompressible case

For an area-preserving flow $\det \nabla F = 1$, so $\lambda_1 \lambda_2 = 1$ everywhere. LCStool's 'incompressible' option enforces this by setting $\lambda_1 \leftarrow 1/\lambda_2$ wherever $\lambda_2 > 1$ (and tagging NaN elsewhere).

Finite-difference computation

On the auxiliary grid, $\nabla F$ is estimated using central differences between four neighbouring image points:

$$ \nabla F \approx \begin{pmatrix} \dfrac{x(t; x_0{+}\delta) - x(t; x_0{-}\delta)}{2\delta} & \dfrac{x(t; y_0{+}\delta) - x(t; y_0{-}\delta)}{2\delta} \\[9pt] \dfrac{y(t; x_0{+}\delta) - y(t; x_0{-}\delta)}{2\delta} & \dfrac{y(t; y_0{+}\delta) - y(t; y_0{-}\delta)}{2\delta} \end{pmatrix} $$

then $C = \nabla F^\top \nabla F$ is assembled entry-wise in compute_cgStrain (internal to eig_cgStrain.m):

$$ C_{11} = F_{11}^2 + F_{21}^2,\quad C_{12} = F_{11}F_{12} + F_{21}F_{22},\quad C_{22} = F_{12}^2 + F_{22}^2. $$

3. Finite-Time Lyapunov Exponent (FTLE) theory

The FTLE is the logarithmic growth rate of the largest finite-time stretch:

$$ \Lambda_{t_0}^{t}(\mathbf{x}_0) \;=\; \frac{1}{|\,t - t_0\,|}\; \ln\!\sqrt{\lambda_2(\mathbf{x}_0)} \;=\; \frac{1}{2\,|t - t_0|}\; \ln \lambda_2(\mathbf{x}_0). $$

This is literally what ftle.m computes:

function ftle_ = ftle(max_eigenvalue, timespan)
    ftle_ = .5*log(max_eigenvalue)/timespan;

Ridges of $\Lambda_{t_0}^{t}$ are a historically popular (though not rigorous) proxy for hyperbolic LCSs. Haller's variational theory below makes the proxy rigorous.

4. Hyperbolic LCS — variational theory theory

Among all smooth material curves $\gamma$ parameterised by $s$, the repelling LCS is one that locally maximises the average normal repulsion rate over the interval $[t_0, t]$. In two dimensions Haller (2011) proved:

Theorem (repelling LCS). A smooth curve $\gamma(s)$ is a repelling LCS over $[t_0,t]$ if and only if at every $\mathbf{x}_0 \in \gamma$,
  1. $\lambda_1(\mathbf{x}_0) \ne \lambda_2(\mathbf{x}_0)$ and $\lambda_2(\mathbf{x}_0) > 1$;
  2. $\gamma'(s)$ is parallel to $\xi_1(\mathbf{x}_0)$ at every point;
  3. $\langle \xi_2,\,\nabla^2 \lambda_2\,\xi_2\rangle \le 0$ (a curvature / ridge condition on $\lambda_2$).

Condition (2) says a repelling LCS is an integral curve of the minor eigenvector field $\xi_1$ — LCStool calls these shrink lines. Condition (3) says the curve must lie on a local ridge of $\lambda_2$; LCStool approximates this by seeding curves at local maxima of $\lambda_2$ on the grid (see seed_curves_from_lambda_max).

Attracting LCSs come from the same theorem applied to the inverse flow map: they are integral curves of the major eigenvector field $\xi_2$, seeded at local minima of $\lambda_1$ (equivalently, local maxima of $-\lambda_1$). These are the stretch lines.

Why integrate a non-orientable field?

Eigenvectors are only defined up to a sign: $\xi_1$ and $-\xi_1$ are both valid. Direct numerical integration would flip orientation at random between cells and produce spurious "breaks" in the curve. LCStool handles this by tracking the previous direction in an ODE-output callback (ode_output in integrate_line.m) and flipping the sign of the new vector whenever the dot product turns negative:

$$ \mathbf{v}_{\mathrm{new}} \leftarrow \operatorname{sign}(\mathbf{v}_{\mathrm{prev}} \cdot \mathbf{v}_{\mathrm{new}})\,\mathbf{v}_{\mathrm{new}}. $$

Grid cells where the orientation change exceeds $\pi/2$ are flagged as orientation discontinuities and the interpolant is locally patched (this is what the helper is_element_with_orient_discont does).

5. Elliptic LCS — coherent vortex theory theory

Haller and Beron-Vera (2013, JFM 731 R4) showed that coherent material vortex boundaries are the outermost closed integral curves $\gamma$ of the generalised Green-Lagrange Lorentzian metric

$$ E_\lambda(\mathbf{x}_0) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\bigl(\,C(\mathbf{x}_0) - \lambda^2 I\,\bigr), \qquad \lambda > 0. $$

Intuitively, along such a curve every infinitesimal material element is stretched by exactly the same factor $\lambda$ — so the curve does not deform tangentially relative to itself. Closed curves with $\lambda = 1$ are literally material lines whose length is preserved from $t_0$ to $t$; nearby curves with $\lambda \ne 1$ act as uniformly stretching/compressing boundaries.

Variational characterisation

Curves along which $E_\lambda$ vanishes tangentially satisfy the null-geodesic condition

$$ \bigl\langle \gamma'(s),\; (C - \lambda^2 I)\,\gamma'(s)\bigr\rangle \;=\; 0. $$

Solving for $\gamma'$ in the $(\xi_1,\xi_2)$ eigenbasis (where $C = \operatorname{diag}(\lambda_1,\lambda_2)$) gives the two η-fields below.

6. λ-lines and the η-vector fields theory

Substituting $\gamma'(s) = a\,\xi_1 + b\,\xi_2$ into the null condition and requiring unit length yields the two solution branches

$$ \boxed{\;\; \eta_\lambda^\pm(\mathbf{x}_0) \;=\; \sqrt{\dfrac{\lambda_2 - \lambda^2}{\lambda_2 - \lambda_1}}\;\xi_1(\mathbf{x}_0) \;\pm\; \sqrt{\dfrac{\lambda^2 - \lambda_1}{\lambda_2 - \lambda_1}}\;\xi_2(\mathbf{x}_0). \;\;}$$

This is exactly Eq. 3.2 of Haller & Beron-Vera (2013) and is what lambda_line.m evaluates. Its MATLAB code is a line-for-line transcription:

etaPos(:,1) = sqrt((l2 - lambda^2)./(l2 - l1)).*xi1(:,1) + sqrt((lambda^2 - l1)./(l2 - l1)).*xi2(:,1);
etaPos(:,2) = sqrt((l2 - lambda^2)./(l2 - l1)).*xi1(:,2) + sqrt((lambda^2 - l1)./(l2 - l1)).*xi2(:,2);
etaNeg(:,1) = sqrt((l2 - lambda^2)./(l2 - l1)).*xi1(:,1) - sqrt((lambda^2 - l1)./(l2 - l1)).*xi2(:,1);
etaNeg(:,2) = sqrt((l2 - lambda^2)./(l2 - l1)).*xi1(:,2) - sqrt((lambda^2 - l1)./(l2 - l1)).*xi2(:,2);
Where η is real. The square roots are real only when $\lambda_1 \le \lambda^2 \le \lambda_2$. Outside this band η becomes complex; LCStool sets those points to NaN when 'forceComplexNaN', true is passed.

Closed λ-lines = elliptic LCS candidates

A closed orbit of $\eta_\lambda^\pm$ around a vortex core is, by construction, a curve of uniform stretching factor $\lambda$. The outermost such closed orbit (at any $\lambda$) is the most coherent material boundary around that vortex.

The Poincaré-section return map

To find closed orbits, LCStool uses a classical Poincaré-section approach. Let $\Sigma$ be a short line segment transverse to the vector field near a suspected vortex core. For an initial point $\mathbf{p}_0 \in \Sigma$, integrate $\eta_\lambda^\pm$ forward until it returns to $\Sigma$ at a point $\mathbf{p}_1$. The return distance is

$$ d(\mathbf{p}_0) \;=\; \operatorname{sgn}\bigl((\mathbf{p}_1 - \mathbf{p}_0)\!\cdot\!\hat{\Sigma}\bigr)\;\|\mathbf{p}_1 - \mathbf{p}_0\|. $$

A closed orbit passes through $\mathbf{p}_0$ iff $d(\mathbf{p}_0) = 0$. LCStool discretises $\Sigma$ with numPoints seeds, finds zero-crossings of $d(\cdot)$ by linear interpolation (the crossing helper in poincare_closed_orbit.m), then refines each to high accuracy with bisection:

$$ \text{if } d(p_1)\,d(p_3) < 0 \text{ then } p_2 \leftarrow p_3, \;\text{else}\; p_1 \leftarrow p_3,\quad p_3 = \tfrac{1}{2}(p_1+p_2). $$

λ-sweep and the outermost orbit

A single $\lambda$ might yield several nested closed orbits. LCStool sweeps $\lambda$ over a user-chosen range (lambda = 0.9:0.02:1.1 in the ocean demo), finds the outermost closed orbit at each, and then elliptic_lcs picks the $\lambda$ that maximises the enclosed area (via polyarea) — that is the declared elliptic LCS for the section.
